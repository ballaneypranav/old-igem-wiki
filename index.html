About iGEM

The World’s Largest Synthetic Biology Competition

The iGEM Foundation is an independent, non-profit organization dedicated to the advancement of synthetic biology via education and competition. It promotes the development of an open community and collaboration through the same.

Teams integrate synthetic DNA components to create novel, engineered organisms to solve various social and biological problems. Their participation is judged on the quality of their biological work, the significance and applicability of the project, human practices and safety components, and the presentation of the work via their website, poster, and a formal oral presentation.

Our Team

The first-ever iGEM team from BITS Pilani University!

We are a team, the first of it's kind across all BITS campuses, of twelve highly motivated and hard-working students with a clear goal in mind. Our team consists of a mix of interdisciplinary students from not only the Biology department but also the departments of Chemical Engineering, Economics, Physics and Computer Science, adding the much-needed diversity in approaching one of the most detrimental problems in agricultural history. iGEM provides our team with a platform to step into the world of research and contribute meaningfully.

The Problem

Post-harvest sugar losses due to invertase in sugarcane

Indian farmers incur damages of up to ₹926.51 billion annually due to post-harvest sucrose losses in sugarcane. Primarily, these losses occur during the transport of sugarcane from the farm to the mill as the enzyme invertase breaks down the sucrose and converts it to glucose and fructose. The activity of invertase increases as time progresses which makes transport of sugarcane to long distances unviable and uneconomical.

Source: ICAR-SBI Sugarcane Breeding Institute, Coimbatore

The above graph shows the amount of sugar retrieved from the sugarcane over the past few decades. The peak efficiency was merely a little over 10.5%. This shows the need of an effective mechanism to curb invertase action. Currently, there are only certain chemical formulations that have been developed with anti-invertase activity but these have not been deployed in the market yet.

Our Solution

Genetically engineered E. coli that inhibit invertase activity

Synthetic biology allows us to manipulate the genetic sequences of certain vectors that allow us to create a commercially viable genetic device. We propose to use these principles to construct an E. coli strain that would solve this pressing issue. We would be using techniques such as PCR, cloning, SDS PAGE and protein assays for showing proof of concept.

Other areas of our project include computational modelling which can predict and evaluate our biological model and a human practices and public relations team that would engage with various stakeholders like research centers dedicated to sugarcane and sugar production and industry experts to create a holistic solution that can be applied at grassroot level.
